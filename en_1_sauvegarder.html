Video games progressively became the ideal place to build worlds drawing on our media heritage. Contemporary video game culture is obsessed with documenting these anachronistic explorations: players can now save their games by livecasting on YouTube, or by taking advantage of the photography feature incorporated into many popular titles. Better yet, new retro game consoles make it possible to revisit our favourite video game worlds, and to get closer to material aspects that have disappeared over time.

It becomes harder, even for a tech savvy audience, to evaluate the authenticity of these hommages. The Game & Watch remakes, which appeared in the 1990s, incorporate characters who became famous afterwards, such as Mario and Yoshi.

Beyond emulation, fan communities find a myriad of other ways to put nostalgia in a box.

In addition to retro-inspired games, new editions of great classics turn up on our virtual storefronts, each one a little more “HD” than the last. Fans invest enormous amounts of time to create emulators of forgotten platforms or filters which simultaneously aim for a higher screen resolution and visual effects throiwing us back to original CRTs. Fragments of this fan labor is often reused by IP owners to create new anniversary editions of classic titles.

In Life is Strange, photography student Maxine Caulfield learns to juggle with time in order to save the small community of Arcadia Bay. The protagonist (and by extension the player) document their path using a vintage Polaroid camera. These snapshots become memory objects which are always accessible in the teenaged girl’s diary.

Fatal Frame II: Crimson Butterfly
2003, Tecmo
Platform : PlayStation 2

To liberate tormented souls in the Fatal Frame franchise, players must learn to master the art of framing and timing with a media relic: the “camera obscura.” Here, photography literally becomes a preservation mechanic.
