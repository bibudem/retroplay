Video games progressively became the ideal place to build worlds drawing on our media heritage. Contemporary video game culture is obsessed with documenting these anachronistic explorations: players can now save their games by livecasting on YouTube, or by taking advantage of the photography feature incorporated into many popular titles. Better yet, new retro game consoles make it possible to revisit our favourite video game worlds, and to get closer to material aspects that have disappeared over time.

Our individual and collective memory is archived on a myriad of technological supports which do not always withstand the test of time. These out-of-date technologies are more present than ever, however, in our nostalgic impulses, inseparable from our desire to rediscover a fantasized childhood or home. Analogue revival. Vintage couture. Camcorder comeback. Vinyl resurgence. Retro gaming. “Technostalgia” is everywhere.

A great number of contemporary "retro" video games build up worlds with a new fundamental brick: overblown pixels. This pixellated aspect is an illusion. It is no longer connected to the technical and material aspects of our TV sets.

In a retro-looking game released twenty-three years later, our protagonist Trace encounters a gigantic bio-mechanical figure inspired by Slavic folklore.

In one corner of this labyrinth, our pixellated avatar discovers a muddled image of himself transmitted over the air on a gigantic television set. Or perhaps he is now facing, in fact, a more faithful reflection of his origins?

"TV Wonderland", a labyrinth constructed with media platforms old and new, is an actual game level that appeared on our screens in 1992. In this platform game, players are invited to control the acrobatics of Harlequin, the sixteenth-century commedia dell’arte jester, recently reincarnated as Harley Quinn in the transmedia world of DC Comics.
